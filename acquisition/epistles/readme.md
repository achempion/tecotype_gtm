# Epistles acquisition report

Research date: **2026-07-17**

Scope: **Epistles Mail at `epistles.com`, its public app-store and package
distribution, and attributable public discussion; older unrelated uses of the
domain are separated from the current product**

Market: **Google US-English, desktop where applicable**

Epistles is a newly generally available, multi-provider email client with unusually
broad platform distribution for its age. Its public acquisition system combines
provider-specific founder posts, a small branded subreddit, app stores and direct
packages, comparison SEO, a two-account free tier, and a capped lifetime offer.

The public evidence connects those activities to discussion, a 100+ Google Play
download band, three US App Store ratings, and a first-party counter that labels
83 lifetime seats “sold.” It does not connect any source to an install, connected
mailbox, useful mail action, return, refund, or paid transaction.

## Executive answer

The strongest evidenced discovery paths are:

1. **Provider-specific founder posts.** Epistles asked Fastmail, Proton,
   de-Google, Stalwart/JMAP, Skiff, Tuta, and existing-email-client communities
   for feedback. The clearest early response was a June 17 Stalwart post with a
   captured score of +9, concrete questions, one request for an invite, and one
   stated intention to join. This is stronger than its generic launch cross-posts.
2. **A small support community with real user reports.** The public
   `r/EpistlesMail` community was created May 2 and showed 82 members on the
   research date. It contains praise, feature requests, setup questions, and
   failures. One independent Stalwart user report scored +4 and said the writer
   intended to buy lifetime if remaining issues were fixed; that is purchase
   intent, not a purchase.
3. **App-store and direct-package availability.** Google Play showed **100+
   downloads**, version 1.3.8, and a July 16 update. The US App Store showed
   version 1.3.8, a 3.67/5 average from three ratings, and an original June 4
   release. macOS, Windows, Android, and four Linux package paths are also
   offered directly, but request counts are not public.
4. **A deliberate but still tiny comparison-SEO footprint.** The sitemap has 25
   indexable pages, including 12 alternative pages. DataForSEO modeled four
   ranking keywords and 0.328 estimated visits. The Mimestream page was the only
   purchase-adjacent result with meaningful visibility: a live check placed it
   at absolute position 8 for `mimestream alternative`.
5. **A visible lifetime offer.** The public counter endpoint returned
   `sold: 83`, `remaining: 417`, and a $79 current tier. If every counted seat
   represented a completed $79 payment, the face-value arithmetic would be
   $6,557 before taxes, fees, refunds, or complimentary seats. The counter is
   operated by Epistles and payments were not independently verified, so neither
   the count nor the arithmetic is revenue evidence.

The strongest lesson is:

> A precise integration proof can earn attention inside a provider community,
> and a free multi-platform download can make trial easy. Broad distribution,
> scarcity counters, and fast patching still do not establish activation,
> retention, trust, or source-level conversion.

## Discovery paths

The most credible beta path is:

```text
Provider-specific problem and implementation claim
→ Fastmail, Proton, de-Google, or Stalwart community
→ questions, criticism, invite request, or waitlist visit
→ private-beta invitation
→ app-store or direct install
→ mailbox connection and real use unobservable
```

The current free path is:

```text
Community mention, exact-brand search, app-store search, or comparison page
→ homepage or store listing
→ free download on one of eight claimed platforms
→ up to two connected accounts, with no account claimed as required to start
→ first successful sync, first useful action, and return unknown
```

The paid paths are:

```text
Free use or pricing CTA
→ Epistles sign-in or account creation
→ 14-day Pro trial
→ $35 annual subscription
→ renewal and retention unknown
```

```text
Pricing or Bereans page
→ $79 / $99 lifetime offer
→ Epistles sign-in or account creation
→ checkout
→ first-party seat counter
→ payment, refund, activation, and retained use unverified
```

## What appears to be working

- **Specificity beats a generic launch.** The Stalwart and Proton discussions
  generated implementation questions because they addressed unusual, costly
  gaps: native JMAP and Proton without Bridge.
- **Distribution breadth removes trial friction.** Store listings and direct
  packages make Epistles reachable without waiting for a platform roadmap.
- **The branded community produces a fast feedback loop.** Changelog entries
  and founder replies connect reports to frequent fixes, even though no
  retention data is visible.
- **One comparison page has reached purchase-adjacent page one.** The
  Mimestream page is an early indication that focused alternative pages can
  rank before a domain has meaningful overall search traffic.
- **The lifetime counter creates a visible commercial narrative.** It is the
  clearest public purchase-related number among Epistles' surfaces, provided it
  is treated as a first-party counter rather than audited revenue.

## What appears weak or risky

- **Source attribution is structurally weak from the outside.** Community posts
  link to plain URLs; download handoffs carry no visible source parameter; app
  stores and package repositories do not expose source-to-activation outcomes.
- **The SEO footprint is mostly inventory, not traffic.** Twelve alternative
  pages currently yield four modeled keywords and a fraction of one estimated
  visit.
- **The broad launch exposed product risk.** From July 8 through July 17, at
  least 15 public versions fixed composing, wrong-message rendering, sync,
  sign-in, Proton, Tuta, IMAP, Microsoft, notifications, and platform UI
  failures. Fast repair is positive; the underlying list is not a stability
  proof.
- **Trust claims conflict across current first-party pages.** The security and
  promises pages refer to readable code and a public client repository, while
  the developer and security pages also say the source is proprietary and not
  public. The promises and lifetime pages say refund and source-release clauses
  are in the Terms; the current Terms do not contain them.
- **Store disclosure conflicts with policy detail.** Google Play says no data
  is collected or shared, while Epistles' own privacy and security pages say
  the service processes account identifiers, authentication and session data,
  some provider tokens, push tokens, support records, and optional tracking
  metadata.
- **Identity surfaces are not fully unified.** The site says Moriah LLC; the US
  App Store seller is Netwarden LLC. This is not evidence of wrongdoing, but it
  adds verification work for a product asking for sensitive mailbox access.

## Implications for Tecotype

- **What appears to be working:** A narrow provider or workflow proof can earn
  thoughtful attention before general category SEO does. Broad free
  availability and a public support loop can then turn awareness into trial.
  Epistles also has one early comparison-SEO result and a first-party
  purchase-related counter worth watching.
- **What appears weak, discontinued, or inconclusive:** General organic search,
  credible editorial referrals, paid acquisition, activation, retention, and
  attributed revenue are not established. The private waitlist and invite gate
  were removed after 1.0. The launch's severe fix list and current
  documentation conflicts make eight-platform availability an ambiguous win.
- **What Tecotype should test before release:** Conduct problem interviews
  around Gmail-plus-IMAP work, keyboard triage, and finding mail from partial
  memory. Record the exact problem and provider mix, but do not offer access
  while the first-run/no-account state, public site, license gate, payment,
  Gmail restricted-API audit, signed distribution, and acquisition
  measurement remain unfinished.
- **What Tecotype should test after the release gate:** Publish one honest
  problem page and one corresponding, disclosed community invitation. Give
  every link and download handoff a source token, then measure installation,
  account connection, first completed sync, the message-matched first-value
  action, voluntary next-day and day-seven return, and payment separately.
  A focused Mimestream-alternative page may be relevant only after release,
  and only if it fairly explains the then-shipped provider scope and Mac-first
  limits.
- **What Tecotype should avoid:** Do not claim availability beyond the tested
  release, launch on many platforms at the expense of core mail correctness,
  use a lifetime counter as demand proof, imply that provider communication
  never occurs, or publish security promises that are absent from the actual
  terms and implementation. Do not reactively match Epistles' $35 price;
  its public price does not expose its retention, costs, or economics.
- **Evidence strength and remaining unknowns:** The pages, metadata, source
  code references, public endpoint, app-store metadata, package manifests,
  subreddit, selected threads, current search results, backlink rows, and
  changelog were directly observed. Actual visitors, post click-through,
  unique installs, connected mailboxes, useful actions, active users,
  cohort retention, refunds, paid transactions, revenue, and acquisition
  attribution remain unavailable.
