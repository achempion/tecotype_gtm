# Epistles history and acquisition chronology

Research date: **2026-07-17**

The `epistles.com` domain has a long unrelated history. This chronology
separates current-product evidence from prior domain uses and distinguishes
first-party claims from independently visible events.

## Domain-history boundary

Wayback records for the domain predate the current product by decades.

- Historical captures reach back to 1998.
- A 2017 capture was an unrelated “Personal Video Epistles” site.
- Old backlinks include immigration content, domain lists, nameservers, and
  unrelated software-business pages.
- The first captured site for the current email client was on 2026-06-18 and
  described a private beta and waitlist.

The current product must not inherit the old domain's backlink age, content,
or apparent history.

## Message evolution

### Private-beta phase

The early outreach emphasized:

- native/provider-specific paths rather than lowest-common-denominator IMAP;
- Proton without Bridge;
- native JMAP for Fastmail and Stalwart;
- direct device-to-provider sync;
- broad desktop and mobile plans; and
- requests for provider-specific edge cases.

The homepage and posts asked users to join a waitlist, and invites were sent in
batches.

### General-availability phase

The current homepage leads with:

- one client for every email account;
- a free two-account tier;
- all eight claimed platforms;
- no Epistles account needed to start;
- local-first mail and on-device search;
- a Cloud Vault with documented server functions; and
- download rather than waitlist.

Pricing, a founding lifetime offer, app stores, direct repositories, a
comparison-page program, and a public security narrative now sit in the same
funnel.

The private waitlist and invite mechanism was explicitly removed after 1.0.

## Release cadence

The official changelog plus the current direct manifests show **at least 15
public versions** from 1.0 on July 8 through direct build 1.3.9 on July 17.

The patch sequence addressed material areas including:

- compose actions;
- showing the wrong message body;
- sign-in and session behavior;
- initial and incremental sync;
- Proton and Tuta authentication;
- IMAP and Microsoft behavior;
- notifications;
- attachments;
- calendar/contact discovery;
- platform-specific layout and interaction; and
- upgrade and trial flow.

This cadence supports two conclusions at once:

1. the team can respond and distribute fixes quickly; and
2. general availability exposed serious core-mail and account-connection
   defects.

It does not establish that every reported issue is resolved or that current
users are retained.

## Store and package chronology

- Apple metadata places the first public store release on June 4, before the
  July 8 changelog's 1.0 general-availability date.
- Google Play showed 100+ downloads and current version 1.3.8.
- Apple and Google listings were at 1.3.8 on the research date.
- The direct Mac and Windows repositories were already at 1.3.9.
- The public changelog still ended at 1.3.8.

These are normal distribution lags in isolation. Together they mean “current
version” depends on platform and channel.

## Trust narrative changes and drift

Current public pages contain several states from different product phases:

- homepage schema says general availability in May 2026;
- the June 18 Wayback page was still private beta;
- the changelog dates 1.0 to July 8;
- `robots.txt` still describes pricing as unreleased;
- the developer page places a chronological unified list later in 2026, while
  current pricing, schema, subreddit copy, and changelog say unified inbox is
  available;
- parts of the security page remain in future tense after launch; and
- the same security narrative says both that code can be read and that the
  source is proprietary and not public.

The chronology explains some drift, but does not resolve it. Current
acquisition claims still need to be internally consistent.

## CASA claim boundary

The July 17 homepage says Epistles passed a CASA Tier 2 assessment by TAC
Security.

- Epistles' badge and statement are directly observed.
- The App Defense Alliance lists TAC Security as an authorized assessor.
- No Epistles-specific assessment letter, scope, date, report, findings, or
  remediation artifact was obtained in the narrow public search.

The correct historical statement is therefore that the claim appeared on the
current homepage—not that the assessment result was independently verified.

## Acquisition interpretation

Epistles used private beta as provider research, then converted quickly to a
broad free launch with:

- eight claimed platforms;
- store and direct distribution;
- provider-community posts;
- a branded support community;
- comparison SEO;
- annual and lifetime pricing; and
- very frequent patches.

That sequence created attention, trials, questions, and at least one
first-party purchase-related count. It also concentrated stability and trust
risk into the first public week.

For Tecotype, speed should mean short learning loops inside a truthful release
boundary. It should not mean presenting planned providers, unverified privacy
assurances, or unfinished first-run/payment paths as shipped.
