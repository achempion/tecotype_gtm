# Wave 1 acquisition synthesis

Research date: **2026-07-17**

Scope: **the ten direct and comparable competitors in Wave 1**

This synthesis compares:

- [Tatem](./tatem/readme.md)
- [Quartz](./quartz/readme.md)
- [Carbon Mail](./carbon-mail/readme.md)
- [Mimestream](./mimestream/readme.md)
- [ZenMail](./zenmail/readme.md)
- [Epistles](./epistles/readme.md)
- [YouniqMail](./youniqmail/readme.md)
- [Velo](./velo/readme.md)
- [Castellum](./castellum/readme.md)
- [Locker](./locker/readme.md)

Every metric is a point-in-time public observation, provider estimate,
first-party statement, or individual anecdote. No competitor exposed a
complete source-to-retained-customer attribution model.

## Executive answer

Wave 1 supports one durable conclusion:

> A precise story can earn attention. The acquisition system becomes credible
> only when a reliable product converts that attention into first value,
> voluntary return, and payment.

Mimestream is the strongest model because its narrow Gmail-on-Mac promise,
relevant founder credibility, long free beta, independent Mac coverage, and
current category search all reinforce one another. Even Mimestream does not
publish source-level activation, retention, or customer economics.

The other clients expose useful parts of a system:

- Velo and Quartz show that a concentrated launch story can create a large
  attention burst.
- Epistles shows that provider-specific implementation proofs can produce
  unusually useful questions and early trials.
- YouniqMail shows that an early client can build meaningful search inventory,
  although broad educational traffic is not the same as product-buying intent.
- Tatem shows visible waitlist interest alongside long waits and stale access.
- Carbon shows that founder attention and free access can coexist with
  founder-reported next-day reversion to Gmail among an undefined tester
  cohort.
- ZenMail, Castellum, and Locker show activity or availability without enough
  evidence of a repeatable acquisition engine.

The immediate Tecotype priority is not a broad launch, many comparison pages,
or a lower price. It is to finish a trustworthy Mac release, validate one
frequent job, instrument the entire path privately, and run one small
source-tagged invitation.

## What appears to work

### 1. One specific reason to care

The strongest messages are narrow enough to question:

- Mimestream: native Gmail behavior on Mac.
- Epistles: native JMAP or Proton without Bridge.
- Velo: a fully open-source desktop client built through an unusual AI-assisted
  process.
- Carbon: the human cost of Google's compliance path.
- Tatem: an accessible alternative to an expensive or unavailable incumbent.

These stories earned discussion because they contained a mechanism, identity,
or conflict. Generic “better email” posts and copied launch messages produced
weaker evidence.

For Tecotype, the candidate mechanisms remain:

- find mail from partial or cross-language memory;
- calm keyboard triage; and
- Gmail plus IMAP in one Mac-first client.

Interviews should decide which is frequent and switch-worthy before public
distribution.

### 2. Credibility compounds a narrow promise

Mimestream's relevant founder background, multi-year beta, independent
reviewers, and stable message reinforce each other. Velo's public repository
made a technically unusual build story inspectable. Epistles' technically
specific replies made some provider discussions useful even when the reaction
was skeptical.

The lesson is not to manufacture founder mythology. It is to give a reviewer
or user concrete, current proof:

- who built it and why;
- what the released product does;
- what data moves where;
- which limitations remain; and
- how somebody can verify the claim.

### 3. Friction-light evaluation helps

Mimestream, Quartz, Epistles, YouniqMail, Velo, Castellum, and Locker place a
download or release path close to the primary message. That removes waitlist
and sales-call friction.

It does not remove product friction. Public evidence includes:

- signing and first-launch trouble;
- OAuth and provider-connection trouble;
- compose and sync failures;
- large or additional model setup;
- unclear account and credential paths; and
- rapidly changing release states.

A direct download is useful only if the first session reaches dependable value.

### 4. Search works when intent and proof match

The three useful search patterns are:

- Mimestream's durable category and brand footprint around a narrowly defined
  product;
- Epistles' early page-one Mimestream-alternative result; and
- YouniqMail's page-one local-first article.

YouniqMail also demonstrates the limit: hundreds of modeled keywords and more
than 1,100 ETV are dominated by broad definitions such as CC and BCC. That
inventory may create awareness or link equity, but it is far from a paid-client
decision.

Tecotype should publish one release-accurate page for one observed job. It
should not scale a generic content program from keyword volume alone.

## What does not establish an acquisition engine

Across the wave, none of these is sufficient:

- a Product Hunt rank;
- Reddit points or comments;
- GitHub stars;
- a waitlist count;
- store download bands;
- release-asset requests;
- a scarcity or “sold” counter;
- many versions;
- many pages or modeled keywords;
- a free beta;
- a low annual or lifetime price; or
- first-party “used by” language without a cohort definition.

Each can be a useful intermediate signal. None shows that the right person
connected a real mailbox, received value, returned voluntarily, and paid.

## Repeated risks

### Attention ahead of reliability

Tatem showed public waitlist interest alongside long waits and stale access.
Carbon's founders reported next-day reversion to Gmail among an undefined
tester cohort. Epistles launched broadly and then repaired serious compose,
sync, authentication, and platform failures at high speed. Velo generated a
large technical launch discussion while signing and setup issues were
immediately visible.

The pattern is consistent: launch attention magnifies the current product,
including its defects.

### Privacy language wider than the data path

Several pages use variants of “never leaves your device” while the product
must communicate with an email provider, an optional model provider, a sync
service, or another explicitly described system.

Other recurring conflicts include:

- analytics disclosed in code but softened in copy;
- store privacy declarations that differ from first-party policies;
- closed source described beside readable-code promises;
- source-release or refund promises absent from Terms;
- signing/notarization claims without a public verification artifact; and
- shipped, beta, and roadmap states mixed on one page.

For an email client, these are acquisition defects because trust is part of the
purchase and authorization decision.

### Weak source continuity

Some clients use tagged directory or launch links, but no public evidence shows
source identity carried through the complete path:

```text
landing
→ download
→ first launch
→ account connection
→ first sync
→ first useful action
→ voluntary return
→ payment
```

The absence of public attribution does not prove private measurement is absent.
It does mean the visible evidence cannot answer which channel works.

### Price without economics

Observed current offers include free beta/open-source access, Mimestream at
$49.99/year, Epistles at $35/year plus first-party $79/$99 lifetime tiers, and
Locker's first-party $39.99 founder-lifetime offer. The billing periods,
entitlements, and product maturity are not normalized. No comparable early
client in the ten dossiers publicly exposes the retention, support burden,
provider-maintenance cost, refund rate, or contribution margin required to
infer an optimal price.

Tecotype should keep its decided **€149/year or €15/month** position and test
whether the complete professional experience earns it. Competitor discounts
are not evidence to move down-market.

## Recommended Tecotype sequence

### Now: validate the job without offering unfinished access

Run 8–10 problem interviews across:

- several Gmail / Workspace accounts;
- Gmail plus IMAP;
- keyboard-oriented Mac users; and
- people who regularly recover mail from partial or cross-language memory.

Ask for a recent real incident, current workaround, frequency, failure cost,
and switching attempt. Show only clearly labeled development material.

Proceed with one lead job only if at least five participants independently
describe the same frequent problem and at least three recently tried to solve
it.

### Before public acquisition: complete the release gate

The gate should include:

- coherent first-run and no-account behavior;
- accurate public site and screenshots;
- licensing and payment;
- Gmail restricted-API/audit completion;
- signed, notarized, updateable Mac distribution;
- safe and predictable compose, reply, and send behavior;
- claim-by-claim privacy/security verification; and
- privacy-minimal source-to-activation measurement.

MCP and agent-driven automation remain future work and must not enter current
acquisition copy.

### First released test: one invitation, one job, one source

Publish one transparent founder invitation in the community closest to the
validated job. Disclose affiliation, Mac-first scope, current providers,
price, data path, and limitations. Give the source a durable identifier.

Measure:

```text
qualified session
→ download
→ first launch
→ account connected
→ first completed sync
→ message-matched first-value action
→ voluntary day-one return
→ voluntary day-seven return
→ paid conversion
```

Do not repeat the post or broaden the message unless at least five non-team
users reach first value and at least three return voluntarily on day seven.
Those are learning thresholds, not statistical proof.

### Second test: a small independent-reviewer brief

After the first cohort can use the product reliably, prepare one factual brief
for a small number of relevant Mac and productivity reviewers:

- one-sentence category;
- founder motivation;
- shipped-capability matrix;
- data-flow diagram;
- keyboard and Better search demonstrations;
- known limitations;
- clean installation and update instructions; and
- no request for editorial control.

Mimestream makes this the highest-confidence expansion path in the wave.

### Third test: one switching/search page

Turn the winning job into one page. A Mimestream comparison is appropriate
only if the then-current product makes the comparison genuinely useful and the
copy accurately states both products' scope.

Primary success is an attributed day-seven or paid user. Rank and click-through
are diagnostic signals.

### Broad launch only after the funnel holds

Product Hunt, larger founder audiences, launch directories, and additional
communities should amplify a path already proven to reach first value and
return. Quartz demonstrates the potential attention; Carbon, Epistles, Tatem,
and Velo demonstrate the cost of amplifying friction.

## Measurement and economics

Tecotype's current model allows roughly **€0.149–€0.50 in pre-cost acquisition
spend per relevant visitor**, depending on the conversion assumption. Every
experiment should retain:

- production and distribution cost;
- relevant sessions;
- downloads and first launches;
- successful account connections and syncs;
- first-value actions;
- day-one and day-seven retained users;
- paid customers;
- net cash after tax, fees, and refunds; and
- support burden.

Scale only on retained-customer economics. Do not optimize the top of the
funnel in isolation.

## Research boundary

The dossiers use current public pages, rendered landing states, metadata,
public APIs, app-store and release metadata, archives, search/backlink
estimates, and public discussions.

No client was installed or launched. Before the user clarified a
no-client-download boundary, Quartz and Mimestream disk images had been
downloaded for read-only static inspection. Neither was executed or installed,
both temporary files were removed on 2026-07-17, and all subsequent work used
public web and metadata only. No other client package was downloaded.

Not available for any complete comparison:

- competitor analytics;
- source-level conversion;
- connected-mailbox counts;
- first-value completion;
- comparable active-user definitions;
- cohort retention;
- verified net paid seats;
- refunds and churn;
- contribution margin; or
- customer-acquisition cost.

Read each linked dossier for source, date, request, and claim-specific
limitations.
