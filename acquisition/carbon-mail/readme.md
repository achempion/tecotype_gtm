# Carbon Mail acquisition report

Research date: **2026-07-17**

Scope: **Carbon Mail at `carbonmail.app`; unrelated products using the same
name are excluded**

Market: **Google US-English, desktop where applicable**

Carbon Mail is a young Gmail browser client built by two pseudonymous founders
who described themselves in January as 17 and 15 years old. Their public
activity provides unusually candid but unlinked evidence about visible launch
attention and a separate tester-retention difficulty.

This report completes the unauthenticated public path through Carbon's own
pre-Google security explanation. No Gmail account was authorized, Google's
unverified-app path was not crossed, and post-authorization onboarding was not
observed.

## Executive answer

The strongest public evidence of how people discover Carbon is:

1. **A founder story about age and Google's compliance cost.** A January
   r/NoCodeSaaS post showed a capture-time score of +19 and prompted advice,
   early-access interest, and offers of financial help.
2. **Repeated founder beta posts.** At least six live near-identical posts
   appeared across four startup subreddits, with visible scores from +1 to +6.
   At least four additional submissions were removed or filtered.
3. **One AI-tool directory.** DataForSEO found four backlinks from one
   referring domain, a Brazilian directory that restates Carbon's claims.
4. **Precise brand search and one community/category intersection.** The
   homepage ranked first organically for `carbon mail email client`; a founder
   Reddit post ranked second for `on-device AI email client`, while the product
   domain was absent from that category result.

The founder/compliance story earned more visible attention than the product
launch. No public evidence connects either to Gmail connection or retained use.
Separately, the founders reported that people liked demonstrations but returned
to Gmail the next morning.

The most defensible lesson is:

> A specific story can create discovery, and free access can remove payment
> friction, but neither proves that a new primary inbox becomes a habit.

Tecotype should measure first value, voluntary next-day return, and day-seven
use before expanding distribution.

## What Carbon tried and changed

- Around January 16, Carbon used a waitlist and manually approved Google test
  users. The founders described only a handful of mostly friends-and-family
  testers.
- On January 19, the founders led with the obstacle of funding a Google
  compliance assessment. That story had Carbon's strongest visible community
  response.
- The first attention burst hit a broken mobile page, waitlist, and desktop
  demo. The founder said the mobile issue was fixed the next day.
- On January 27, the founders cross-posted a beta and retention request across
  several startup communities and said they had spent $0.
- They openly considered changing the category from new client to better Gmail
  interface and building a redirecting extension because testers returned to
  Gmail.
- By the February 2 archive, the site used the current direct Gmail-connect
  path, time-led positioning, broad feature promise, and permanent-free-tier
  pledge.
- The current page calls Carbon a “lens” for Gmail, which appears intended to
  reduce replacement anxiety.
- The current flow still tells users to proceed past Google's unverified-app
  warning and says CASA Tier 2 review is underway.

These changes are observed. Their conversion and retention effects are not.

## Implications for Tecotype

- **What appears to be working:** A specific, candid founder constraint earned
  the strongest visible attention. One product-focused founder post also
  appeared for an on-device category query in the captured SERP. This proves
  visible engagement and search presence, not product acquisition.
- **What appears weak, discontinued, or inconclusive:** The waitlist and manual
  test-user gate disappeared. The repeated beta posts had low scores, and some
  submissions were removed or filtered; the reasons and downstream value are
  unknown. Current non-brand domain visibility, backlinks, directories, paid
  acquisition, partnerships, X activity, payment, and source-level conversion
  are sparse, self-reported, or unknown.
- **What Tecotype should test now:** Use original, community-native research
  around one concrete proof: keyboard triage or finding mail from remembered
  clues. Ask for a research conversation or invited-pilot interest and state
  Apple Silicon, Gmail/IMAP, and local-processing boundaries precisely.
- **What Tecotype should test after the release gate:** Run a small invited
  cohort through account connection, first sync, a source-matched keyboard or
  search action, voluntary next-day return, and day-seven use. Only then test
  one source from visit through retained activation.
- **What Tecotype should avoid:** Do not duplicate promotional posts, count
  votes or OAuth as success, force return through a redirect, copy Carbon's
  free-tier pledge, dramatize inbox pain, promise time savings, use vague
  social proof, or say mail never leaves the device.
- **Evidence strength and remaining unknowns:** Founder activity, page behavior,
  OAuth scopes, current SERPs, and one referring domain are directly observed.
  Tester size, return behavior, and $0 spend are first-party statements.
  Actual visits, waitlist conversion, Gmail connection, activation, retention,
  payments, revenue, and channel attribution remain unavailable.

Detailed experiment designs are in [opportunities.md](./opportunities.md).
