## Archived landing-page changes

The following captures preserve four useful stages:

- [2020-05-03: invitation waitlist](https://web.archive.org/web/20200503165915/https://mimestream.com/)
- [2020-09-10: public beta](https://web.archive.org/web/20200910163943/https://mimestream.com/)
- [2022-09-30: mature free beta](https://web.archive.org/web/20220930161807/https://mimestream.com/)
- [2023-05-22: paid 1.0 launch](https://web.archive.org/web/20230522212137/https://mimestream.com/)

### Invitation period

The May 2020 page used a Mailchimp form and asked visitors to “Request Access.” It said people would join a waitlist for an invitation to the beta. That is observed demand capture; neither the size of the list nor the speed of access is public.

### Public and mature beta

By September 2020, the primary message was the exact category: “A native macOS email client for Gmail.” The page asked visitors to “Join the Beta.” By September 2022 it explicitly said the product was free during beta, required macOS 11 or newer, and offered extensive feature proof.

The free period lasted more than two years. At 1.0, Mimestream said it had:

- released more than 220 updates;
- made more than 2,500 improvements;
- added more than 100 features;
- grown from one developer to a five-person team; and
- had more than 167,000 users join the beta.

These are first-party launch claims. They show accumulated participation and product work, not an attributable cohort or retained-user count.

### Paid 1.0

The May 2023 page changed the hero to “Made for Mac. Optimized for Gmail,” placed “Download Free Trial” above the fold, added a Product Hunt badge, and promoted Mimestream 1.0. Pricing became $49.99 annually or $4.99 monthly after a 14-day no-card trial.

The transition also created friction. The [2023 Hacker News thread](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=36033184) contains people who said they paid or found the product worth its price, alongside complaints about subscription pricing, the beta-expiry schedule, and communication. That is a small, self-selected public sample—not conversion-rate research—but it exposes a trust risk in turning a large free beta into a deadline-driven subscription.

## What the sequence supports

Observed:

- Mimestream stayed tightly focused on native Mac plus Gmail.
- Founder-led technical explanation preceded broad public-beta attention.
- Free beta access and repeated releases preceded the 167,000+ cumulative claim.
- Independent Mac coverage continued across several years.
- A trial, subscription, and launch promotion appeared at 1.0.

Reasonable inference:

- The founder’s Apple Mail background helped reviewers and technical communities understand why this team could build the product.
- Free beta access lowered the initial adoption barrier and gave reviewers time to form opinions.
- Stable category language likely helped press links and current search rankings compound around one meaning.

Unobservable:

- Which source produced the beta audience.
- How many beta participants connected Gmail, became active, or remained active.
- Whether launch discounts increased long-run paid conversion.
- Whether press, search, Product Hunt, or Hacker News had the best economics.
- Why specific landing-page or offer changes were made.

## Interpretation rules

- “Joined the beta” is not equivalent to active, retained, or paid.
- “Hundreds of thousands have used” is not a current customer count.
- Current search visibility does not reconstruct the 2020–2023 source mix.
- Community votes and comments measure visible attention.
- A favorable review can establish credible independent proof, but not conversion.
- A promotion’s existence cannot establish incrementality.
