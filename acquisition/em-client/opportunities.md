# eM Client-derived opportunities for Tecotype

Research date: **2026-07-17**

These are experiments, not commitments or shipped capabilities. They translate
eM Client's public acquisition evidence into smaller tests consistent with
Tecotype's north star: equanimity, calm, clarity, and control.

No eM Client artifact was obtained. The recommendations are based on public
web, store, search, backlink, mention, and advertising evidence only.

## Tecotype truth that gates every experiment

Current repository evidence supports:

| Topic | Current truth |
| --- | --- |
| Product state | Active development |
| Mac | Signed/notarized release path exists; current signed target is Apple Silicon; a public release is not yet established |
| Windows | Signed NSIS, self-hosted, and store distribution are planned, not shipped |
| Accounts | Gmail plus IMAP |
| Core experience | Keyboard-first email, local search, calm control |
| Better search | Optional and on-device in the current verified design |
| Price | €149/year or €15/month |
| MCP, broader intelligence, and agent automation | Future work; not current acquisition claims |

Therefore:

- pre-release tests may research demand and collect consented interest;
- install and activation tests require a genuinely public signed build for the
  named platform;
- Windows availability must not be implied before Windows ships; and
- optional/future capability must not be written as universal current
  behavior.

## What eM Client suggests

eM Client's strongest public mechanisms are not its raw reach claims. They are:

1. explicit licensing and evaluation paths;
2. a partner program with published economics and enablement;
3. a migration motion tied to the Postbox acquisition;
4. durable provider, comparison, support, and release content;
5. active cross-platform distribution;
6. ongoing paid creative; and
7. current product publishing.

Its main warnings are:

- 77.9% of modeled ETV sits in ten provider-help pages;
- four tested broad category queries had no owned eM Client result in the top
  ten;
- link and mention totals are noisy;
- registration and license expiration can interrupt use;
- current consent controls and privacy wording do not align cleanly; and
- no public channel is connected to retained unit economics.

Tecotype should test mechanisms at low volume and demand downstream proof
before scaling them.

## Priority 1: one job-close page

### Hypothesis

A page naming one repeated loss-of-control moment will attract fewer but more
qualified prospects than a broad “best email client/productivity suite”
message.

Candidate problem language must come from interviews, not invention. Examples
to investigate include:

- finding a known thread quickly across a large local mailbox;
- staying in flow with keyboard-driven triage;
- managing Gmail and one additional IMAP account without visual noise; or
- recovering context without sending message content to a remote AI service.

The last statement can be used only to the extent verified for the specific
feature and path. “Private,” “offline,” and “local” must not be generalized
beyond tested behavior.

### Pre-release test

Create a factual demand page with:

- the problem;
- the intended Gmail/IMAP audience;
- a screenshot or demo only if it reflects current behavior;
- exact platform state;
- current planned public price of €149/year or €15/month; and
- one consented research/interview action.

Do not offer a client download before the public Mac release gate.

### Post-Mac-release test

Replace the research action with a signed Mac release CTA only after public
availability is verified. Carry source through:

- setup start;
- first launch;
- privacy explanation;
- account connection;
- successful sync;
- first keyboard or optional on-device Better-search value;
- day-seven voluntary return; and
- payment/refund.

### Success and stop

Success is not page traffic. Require a meaningfully higher connection and
first-value rate than a generic control page, without higher privacy-step
abandonment, refund, or support burden. Stop if the page attracts provider
support intent instead of people evaluating a client.

## Priority 2: five high-intent guides, not a provider farm

### Hypothesis

A small set of truthful, product-adjacent guides can support qualified
discovery without recreating eM Client's thousand-page, login-heavy inventory.

### Test design

Choose at most five pages only after evidence of recurring questions. Suitable
families may include:

- Gmail desktop workflow;
- IMAP compatibility and limits;
- keyboard triage;
- local search behavior;
- optional on-device Better search;
- migration from one verified source; or
- a precise Mac/Windows status page.

Each page should:

- solve the question directly;
- disclose current platform and product limits;
- avoid impersonating a provider login or support page;
- link to one relevant released-product action; and
- be maintained by an owner with a review date.

### Measure

Track qualified CTA intent, successful account connection, first value,
day-seven return, support cost, and paid conversion by page. Search volume and
ETV are secondary.

### Stop

Retire pages that draw login/recovery traffic with no qualified activation,
create provider confusion, or become stale.

## Priority 3: a disclosed specialist-partner pilot

### Hypothesis

A small number of trusted workflow consultants, independent reviewers, or
community educators can deliver higher-intent cohorts than generic affiliate
volume.

### Gate

Recruit for research before release. Activate paid/referral acquisition only
after the relevant signed build is public.

### Pilot

Start with three to five partners. Give them:

- a precise product truth sheet;
- current price and cancellation terms;
- exact Gmail/IMAP and platform support;
- privacy and data-flow boundaries;
- disclosed compensation rules;
- a source identifier;
- escalation/support ownership; and
- permission to report limitations honestly.

Do not require positive coverage or hidden endorsements.

### Measure

Per partner, measure:

- qualified visitors;
- connection and sync success;
- time to first value;
- day-seven return;
- paid conversion;
- refunds;
- support minutes;
- commission; and
- contribution after refunds/support.

### Stop

End partners whose cohorts do not activate, create misleading claims, hide
compensation, or impose disproportionate support cost. Do not scale because a
partner generates clicks.

## Priority 4: migration as a product-backed campaign

### Hypothesis

People leaving one specific client will respond to a calm, precise switching
path if account/import behavior is verified and the destination solves a known
job.

eM Client's Postbox motion worked mechanically because the incumbent was
ending, discounts were explicit, migration content existed, and product
features were adapted. Tecotype should not manufacture urgency or target a
client merely because it is mentioned online.

### Research phase

Interview users who recently switched or want to switch. Identify:

- why they are leaving;
- required providers;
- data and settings that must move;
- keyboard habits;
- expected local/offline behavior;
- trust fears;
- acceptable setup time; and
- willingness to pay €149/year or €15/month.

### Release-gated pilot

After the public Mac release, select one migration source only if:

- source behavior is understood;
- import/connection steps are verified;
- unsupported data is listed;
- rollback/export is clear;
- support capacity exists; and
- source attribution reaches retained value.

Windows migration experiments may be researched now but cannot invite installs
until signed Windows distribution ships.

### Stop

Stop if setup failure, data ambiguity, or support burden exceeds the retained
value of the cohort. Never use “seamless” without evidence.

## Priority 5: release history as acquisition proof

### Hypothesis

A concise, honest release history can reduce the risk of adopting a small
email client more effectively than cumulative user counts.

### After public release

Publish:

- date;
- platform and architecture;
- version;
- signing/notarization state;
- fixes/features actually shipped;
- known limitations;
- security/privacy changes; and
- support/update policy.

Separate Mac and Windows. Keep planned work in a roadmap or preview, not release
notes.

### Measure

Observe whether release-history visitors proceed to a released-platform CTA,
activate, and retain. Also track whether it reduces platform/status support
questions.

## Priority 6: calm price and entitlement explanation

### Hypothesis

Explicit access, renewal, cancellation, update, device, and data-export terms
will improve qualified conversion and reduce refund/support risk even if they
reduce impulse purchases.

### Test

At €149/year or €15/month, test a compact explanation of:

- eligible platforms actually shipped;
- billing period;
- cancellation consequence;
- update entitlement;
- device/account boundaries;
- export/data access after cancellation;
- support; and
- optional on-device Better-search scope.

Do not copy eM Client's offline-until-valid-license ambiguity. Specify what
continues to work and what stops.

### Measure

Qualified checkout intent, conversion, refund, cancellation confusion, support
contacts, and early retention matter more than click-through.

## Priority 7: privacy-coherent attribution

### Hypothesis

Tecotype can preserve useful source-to-value measurement without a broad
third-party marketing stack or confusing consent.

### Minimum design

Use:

- first-party campaign/source identifiers;
- an explicit, narrow event dictionary;
- no message content;
- clear retention;
- easy opt-out/decline;
- separation between essential product operations and optional analytics; and
- documentation that matches runtime behavior.

The essential path to measure is:

```text
source
→ released-platform CTA
→ signed first launch
→ connection
→ sync success
→ first value
→ voluntary return
→ payment/refund
```

### Verification

Before launch:

- test a fresh browser before consent;
- test accept and decline;
- inspect cookies and requests;
- verify product telemetry separately;
- compare policy, banner, and actual behavior; and
- ensure any partner/reviewer attribution respects the same choices.

### Stop

Do not add a vendor unless it answers a specific decision that cannot be
answered safely with first-party measurement.

## Priority 8: paid-search learning after activation is healthy

eM Client's archive proves sustained ad activity, not success. Tecotype should
not use paid media to compensate for a weak activation path.

Only after a released Mac cohort reaches reliable connection, first value, and
day-seven return:

1. test one exact high-intent query;
2. send it to the job-close page, not a generic homepage;
3. use a small fixed budget;
4. exclude provider-login and support terms;
5. measure retained paid users and contribution; and
6. stop if the cohort fails quality thresholds.

Repeat for Windows only after signed Windows distribution is actually public.

## Sequencing

| Phase | Allowed work | Explicitly not yet allowed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Now, pre-release | Interviews, demand pages, message tests, partner discovery, migration research, privacy QA design | Claiming public Mac/Windows availability; install acquisition |
| After verified public signed Mac release | Mac CTA, activation measurement, specialist pilot, narrow migration cohort, release history, small paid test | Windows install claims; future MCP/agent claims |
| After verified signed Windows release | Windows-specific pages, cohorts, partners, and ads with separate measurement | Assuming Mac results transfer automatically |
| Later, after feature verification | Precise claims for additional intelligence or automation | Calling plans shipped before verification |

## Decision rules

Scale only when a channel produces:

- successful connection and sync;
- first useful action;
- voluntary return;
- paid conversion at the published price;
- acceptable refund/cancellation;
- manageable support burden;
- privacy-coherent attribution; and
- positive contribution after channel cost.

Do not scale based on:

- impressions;
- search volume;
- ETV;
- backlinks;
- mentions;
- ad creatives;
- waitlist size;
- setup starts; or
- cumulative downloads.

Those can explain discovery. They do not establish calm, retained value.
