# eM Client traffic and search visibility

Research date: **2026-07-17**

Market: **Google United States, English; live SERPs used desktop Windows**

The figures below are DataForSEO estimates or public-site counts, not eM
Client analytics. Estimated traffic value (`ETV`) is a modeled visit estimate.
It does not establish sessions, unique users, signups, installs, connected
mailboxes, activation, retention, or revenue.

No client artifact was requested or used.

## Search inventory

The public XML sitemap contained **1,005 URLs**, all on
`www.emclient.com`. It included:

- **185 `/blog/` URLs**;
- homepage, features, pricing, licensing, free-license, support, release, and
  comparison surfaces;
- provider and setup pages;
- login, recovery, and how-to guides;
- comparison pages against Outlook, Outlook for Mac, Thunderbird, Postbox,
  Mailbird, and Spark; and
- active 2026 articles plus historical product posts.

The homepage navigation exposed English, German, Spanish, Czech, Italian, and
French routes. The sitemap count refers to the inspected `www` sitemap, not a
complete count of every locale or forum URL.

The inventory shows a long-running content operation. It does not by itself
show that pages are indexed, visited, useful, or commercially productive.

## Content architecture

The public inventory uses several reusable page families:

1. **Provider entry pages:** ISP/webmail login, account recovery, service
   setup, and migration.
2. **Integration pages:** Gmail/Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, Exchange,
   Outlook, and other providers.
3. **Alternative and comparison pages:** Outlook, Thunderbird, Postbox,
   Mailbird, Spark, and platform-specific alternatives.
4. **Feature pages:** AI, encryption, translation, calendar, contacts, tasks,
   notes, snooze, profiles, and teams.
5. **Lifecycle pages:** pricing, free license, licensing, support, release
   history, partner program, and blog.
6. **Owned community:** a long-running public support forum whose threads also
   rank for specific issues.

This architecture gives eM Client many discovery surfaces and internal paths
to product pages. It also risks confusing raw search visibility with qualified
email-client demand.

## Blog freshness

The [blog](https://www.emclient.com/blog) was active in 2026. Current posts
included product previews and help around offline mode, pinning, Spotlight, and
other workflow features. The company has also used the blog for major-version
launches, mobile launches, licensing explanations, migration guides, and
Postbox-user onboarding.

Fresh publishing can:

- capture emerging feature intent;
- educate existing users;
- support release and migration campaigns;
- provide material for partners and press; and
- keep the domain current.

Public pages do not reveal subscriber count, readership, assisted conversion,
or retained cohorts.

## Search strengths

- The domain has meaningful modeled US-English visibility.
- Provider pages rank for very large navigational queries.
- Brand search resolves cleanly to the homepage.
- Integration pages appear for some Gmail and Microsoft 365 client queries.
- The content system supports product, support, migration, partner, and
  lifecycle messaging rather than a blog alone.
- Active publishing and a long release history provide recurring indexing and
  announcement opportunities.

## Search weaknesses

- Approximately 77.9% of modeled domain ETV comes from ten provider-help
  pages.
- The homepage accounts for only about 2.6% of modeled ETV.
- Four tested broad category queries returned no eM Client-owned top-ten
  result.
- Login/search-recovery intent may have low propensity to install or pay.
- Provider-help pages can create support expectations unrelated to eM Client.
- A large page inventory adds maintenance, accuracy, consent, and brand-quality
  cost.
- Current ETV and rank do not expose source-to-activation or source-to-revenue.

## Implications for Tecotype

Tecotype should not pursue traffic volume as an end state. A smaller search
program can be better if it targets a verified job and carries intent through
first value.

A release-gated sequence is:

1. Before release, use interviews and small demand pages to identify one
   recurring Gmail/IMAP job where calm control matters.
2. Publish only pages that resolve that job honestly and can be maintained.
3. Once the signed Mac release is public, connect source to successful mailbox
   connection, sync, first keyboard-driven or optional on-device Better-search
   value, voluntary return, and paid conversion.
4. Research Windows terms now, but do not invite installation or claim
   availability until signed Windows distribution is shipped and verified.
5. Expand content only when a page produces qualified activation or durable
   learning—not because a provider keyword has large search volume.

Useful measures are qualified landing sessions, CTA intent, successful
connection, sync completion, time to first value, day-seven return, paid
conversion, refund, support burden, and source-level contribution. ETV and
rank are discovery metrics only.
