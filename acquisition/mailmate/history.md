# MailMate acquisition history

## January 2010: public beta begins with four prior users

The [first beta announcement](https://blog.freron.com/2010/first-beta-released/)
said only four people had tried MailMate and invited the reader to become user
number five. It warned that the product might not be ready as a primary client
and routed feedback to a mailing list or in-app email.

The original acquisition pattern was already present: precise technical fit,
direct access, founder candor, and user feedback.

## 2011: 1.0 and early independent coverage

[Contemporary coverage](https://www.mactech.com/2011/02/17/mailmate-is-new-mac-os-x-email-client/)
described MailMate 1.0 as a macOS IMAP client with offline operation, advanced
searching, smart mailboxes, and alternative layouts. By 2013,
[Macworld](https://www.macworld.com/article/220615/review-roundup-mac-email-clients.html)
praised its search depth while emphasizing the plain interface and narrow
appeal.

This established the durable external framing: visually austere, unusually
powerful, and intended for people with demanding mail workflows.

## 2013: community-funding experiment

The founder used only the blog, Twitter, and mailing list to ask users for
funding. His
[published result](https://blog.freron.com/2013/results-of-the-experimental-crowd-funding/)
was:

- $13,430 in emailed pledges;
- 131 contributors;
- $50 as the most common pledge;
- 64 current MailMate users; and
- 19 trial users.

He said the two weeks increased downloads and sales considerably but did not
publish those counts. The campaign did not meet its unofficial goal. This is
useful evidence of community intensity, not a scalable acquisition cost or
current revenue model.

## 2020: last public release

[MailMate 1.13.2](https://blog.freron.com/2020/mailmate-1-13-2-released/) was
released August 28, 2020 as a security update. The post openly discussed an
S/MIME problem, the need for users to update, and work on a safer message view.

The current download page still identifies r5673 as the public release and
recommends the beta to new users. A long interval without a stable release
weakens acquisition even when development continues privately or through
betas.

## December 2024: homepage return, 2.0 beta, and new pricing

On December 22, 2024, the founder wrote that the homepage was back after almost
two years and the latest public release was more than four years old.

The [2.0 beta](https://blog.freron.com/2024/mailmate-2-0-beta-released/)
bumped the revision to r6067 and became the primary download.

The simultaneous
[license-model announcement](https://blog.freron.com/2024/new-license-key-system/)
said:

- long release intervals were bad for new-user revenue;
- monetization uncertainty contributed to the delay;
- the first license sold more than 14 years earlier still worked;
- eight years of patronage had never surpassed license sales but helped keep
  development alive;
- $10-per-quarter subscriptions would fund development;
- cancelled subscriptions would enter Free Mode; and
- support was not part of the payment guarantee.

The founder described the change as a gamble. No public follow-up quantifies
its effect on revenue.

## July 2025: legacy keys enter Free Mode

On July 1, 2025, most older license keys switched to Free Mode. The
[explanation](https://blog.freron.com/2025/why-did-mailmate-switch-to-free-mode-today/)
addressed surprise, prior free-upgrade expectations, subscriptions, and a
temporary way to remain on the old model.

The post demonstrates responsive transparency, but also shows that the model
was not self-explanatory and produced user confusion.

## Latest publicly linked beta evidence

The current beta release-note link resolved on the capture date to
[revision 6272](https://updates.mailmate-app.com/release_notes_archive/MailMate_r6272.html),
dated July 2, 2025. The
[accompanying post](https://blog.freron.com/2025/another-mailmate-2-0-beta-released/)
says MailMate is a solo-developer business and notes ongoing provider/OAuth
risk.

No claim is made here that development ended on that date. It is simply the
latest public beta evidenced by the current first-party link during this
research.

## What endured

Across more than sixteen years:

- macOS and IMAP remain the platform boundary;
- keyboard control, search, Markdown, smart mailboxes, standards, and security
  remain the message;
- direct evaluation and public feedback remain central;
- users and adjacent Mac tools supply advocacy;
- the founder communicates technical risk candidly; and
- revenue and support capacity remain visible constraints.

This continuity creates trust with a narrow audience. Stale stable releases
and unresolved monetization make it difficult to infer a repeatable growth
engine.
