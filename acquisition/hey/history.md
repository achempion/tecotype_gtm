# HEY acquisition history

## Timeline

### Before launch: two years of product work and a waitlist

[TechCrunch's launch report](https://techcrunch.com/2020/06/16/basecamp-launches-hey-a-hosted-email-service-for-neat-freaks/)
said HEY had been developed for two years and reported more than 50,000 people
on the waitlist immediately before launch. The figure came through launch
reporting and does not define unique, qualified, or activated prospects.

The pre-launch promise was not “another inbox.” It was a new provider and a
complete philosophy, already demonstrated through founder material.

### June 15–16, 2020: concentrated public launch

HEY launched on June 15, 2020. Its
[Product Hunt record](https://www.producthunt.com/products/hey) shows number 1
for the day, week, and month. Launch reporting covered the $99 annual price,
14-day trial at that time, Screener, Imbox, Feed, Paper Trail, tracking
protection, no IMAP/POP, and no historical import.

That completeness gave coverage a large number of concrete hooks. It also
made the switching cost visible from day one.

### June 2020: App Store dispute becomes the story

Apple rejected an iOS update because the app did not provide in-app purchase
or enough utility to a non-subscriber. 37signals publicly contested the
decision. The current [Apple vs. HEY archive](https://www.hey.com/apple/)
links extensive coverage from The Verge, BBC, CNBC, EFF, WIRED, Protocol,
Axios, TechCrunch, and other publications.

[TechCrunch reported](https://techcrunch.com/2020/06/22/apple-approves-hey-bug-fix-update-after-basecamp-agrees-to-tweak-app-at-center-of-store-policy-spat/)
that Apple approved an update after HEY added a free temporary-address
function for non-paying users. The dispute likely amplified awareness, but
public evidence cannot isolate the signups it caused or the users it repelled.

### First 11 days: 125,000 signups

On July 6, 2020,
[Jason Fried told TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2020/07/06/oh-hey/) that
HEY received **125,000 signups in its first 11 days**.

This is the strongest historic funnel number and still has important limits:

- it is a first-party statement carried by an independent publication;
- “signup” is not defined;
- it does not report successful mailbox use;
- it does not separate sources; and
- it does not disclose trial-to-paid conversion or renewal.

### Product expansion

The launch product was personal email. Custom domains and business use were
described as later work. The current offer now includes HEY for Domains and
Families.

In January 2024, DHH wrote that the team had spent the previous year on the
most-requested addition, [HEY Calendar](https://world.hey.com/dhh/new-year-new-calendar-58d70855).
The homepage and pricing now make email plus calendar the top-level category.
This broadens recurring value, but no public cohort shows its effect on
acquisition or renewal.

### Current funnel

The current personal trial is **30 days rather than the launch-era 14 days**,
with no card. Current pricing remains $99/year for a normal personal address,
while domain and family offers add expansion paths.

The company continues to use the same main acquisition assets:

- HEY Way manifesto;
- founder-led product walkthrough;
- opinionated feature pages;
- company and founder distribution;
- platform availability;
- migration education; and
- a clear paid, ad-free business model.

## What changed and what endured

Changed:

- trial length increased from 14 to 30 days;
- waitlist/invite access became open signup;
- custom domains and families were added;
- calendar became part of the lead category; and
- the App Store workaround ended the immediate distribution block.

Endured:

- $99 annual personal price;
- provider rather than generic-client architecture;
- no IMAP/POP;
- no historical-mail import;
- strong founder voice;
- privacy-for-payment framing; and
- the named HEY workflow.

The public record is rich on launches and product decisions but thin on
present acquisition efficiency. No source reviewed exposes current new trials,
activation, paid conversion, churn, or renewal.
