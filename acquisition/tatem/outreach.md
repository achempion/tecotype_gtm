# Founder outreach and audience-transition opportunity

Research date: **2026-07-17**

This file evaluates whether Tecotype should approach Tatem about reaching its email-era audience. Tecotype does not need Tatem’s code or brand, so a whole-project acquisition is not the default recommendation.

## Current founder and ownership picture

Christopher LaFerla founded Tatem. Public evidence now places him at Wander:

- [Christopher LaFerla’s LinkedIn profile](https://www.linkedin.com/in/christopherlaferla) currently lists Wander and San Francisco, California.
- In October 2025, [Wander’s founder described Christopher as a former Founder & CEO](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/jaentwistle_at-wander-every-executive-should-operate-activity-7389412193173295104-qfQI), said he would lead Wander’s product designers and design engineers, and said the Tatem team had joined him.
- Former Tatem team members [Elwin Wang](https://elwinwang.com/) and [Lauren Dorman](https://laurendorman.io/) describe Tatem as acquired by Wander.
- A [corporate-record mirror](https://www.bizprofile.net/ca/irvine/tatem-inc) reported Tatem Inc. active and registered in Irvine, California as of July 15, 2025, before the Wander announcement. That filing snapshot does not establish current asset ownership.

The safest conclusion is:

> Tatem appears to have been acquired or acquihired by Wander, but the public announcement does not disclose whether Wander acquired the company, code, brand, domain, user accounts, marketing list, or only the team.

Christopher is the correct first conversation, but he may not be able to authorize an audience campaign alone. Tecotype should ask whether Tatem Inc. or Wander now controls the relevant communication and contractual rights.

The location evidence is inconsistent across public sources. The current LinkedIn profile says San Francisco; older company and directory records point to Irvine or Los Angeles. Treat **California** as reliable and use digital contact routes rather than a physical address.

## What Tecotype should propose

The preferred offer is an **opt-in audience-transition campaign**, not a purchase of Tatem’s contact list:

```text
Tatem or Wander sends an accurate message
→ email-era recipient learns about Tecotype
→ recipient explicitly opts in to Tecotype
→ Tecotype landing page or release flow
→ download
→ successful account connection
→ chosen activation event
```

Recommended structure:

- Tatem or Wander remains the sender.
- Start with a small, clearly defined email-era cohort.
- Explain the relationship and both products’ status accurately.
- Recipients choose whether to hear from or try Tecotype.
- Tecotype receives only people who explicitly opt in.
- Compensation can combine a limited setup amount with a performance component based on qualified opt-ins or activated users.
- Expand only if the first cohort produces qualified activation without trust or deliverability problems.

This structure tests whether Tatem’s audience is relevant while avoiding the liabilities of buying a company Tecotype does not otherwise need.

## Privacy and data boundary

Tatem’s [privacy policy](https://tatem.com/privacy) says:

- Tatem does not sell personal information to third parties.
- Marketing messages to non-account holders require consent.
- Information may transfer during a sale, merger, bankruptcy, asset sale, or reorganization, with notice and the policy’s promises continuing to apply.
- Google user data is not sold or used for advertising and is shared only in narrow service-provider or legal circumstances.

Therefore:

- A raw audience-list purchase is not a supported default.
- A company or asset transaction would not automatically give Tecotype unrestricted marketing rights.
- Tecotype should not request or accept Gmail content, Google authorization tokens, contacts, calendars, or other product data.
- The lowest-risk experiment is a campaign sent by the current rights holder with explicit Tecotype opt-in.
- Counsel should verify consent, notice, and territorial requirements before any data or marketing arrangement is signed.

This is an operational research conclusion, not legal advice.

## Required diligence before discussing value

The public “10,000+” claim should have no acquisition value until the cohort is defined. Tatem reused the same domain and app across several products.

Ask for:

| Question | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Who owns the audience and right to contact it after the Wander transaction? | Determines the real counterparty |
| How many records belong specifically to Tatem Email? | Removes operations/project-management contamination |
| How many are active users, former users, waitlist contacts, or general marketing subscribers? | Separates product use from low-intent signup |
| When was each cohort last contacted? | Predicts list decay and complaint risk |
| What were the last deliverability, open, click, and unsubscribe results? | Tests whether the audience is reachable |
| What consent language and preferences were recorded? | Defines permissible communication |
| What countries are represented? | Identifies different legal requirements |
| How many match Tecotype’s supported platform and account providers? | Estimates relevance |
| Can Tatem or Wander send a small test without transferring the list? | Enables validation before commitment |
| Are investors, contracts, or the Wander deal relevant to approval? | Exposes consent and transaction dependencies |

Do not value the opportunity from list size. Value it from observed, consented, qualified activation.

## Recommendation

Approach Christopher, but do not open with an offer to buy Tatem.

Ask for a short exploratory conversation covering:

1. Current product and audience status.
2. Ownership after Wander.
3. Whether an honest, Tatem-sent introduction is possible.
4. Whether a small email-era cohort can be tested first.

Only consider a broader transaction if the test proves relevant activation and the necessary rights cannot be licensed more simply. Because Tecotype does not need the code or brand, buying Tatem Inc. or its full asset set would add diligence, privacy, contractual, investor, and historical-product liabilities without a clear corresponding benefit.
