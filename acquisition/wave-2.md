The historical competitor conclusions remain useful supporting evidence.

Every metric is a point-in-time public observation, a provider estimate, a
first-party statement, or an individual anecdote. No client published a
complete source-to-retained-customer attribution model. No client software was

Under Tecotype's evidence standard, none of these routes counts as proven
distribution: no public source joins permanent discovery to an attributable
activation-to-paid funnel. “Visible” below means a durable public discovery,
evaluation, or measurement surface, not demonstrated customer acquisition.

## Executive answer

Wave 2 does not reveal one acquisition playbook to copy. It reveals a sequence:

> Earn attention with a specific reason to switch, reduce the risk of changing
> a primary inbox, help the right person reach first value, and only then
> compound distribution through search, stores, partners, referrals, launches,
> or paid media.

The most transferable evidence comes from different clients at different
stages:

- **Superhuman** provides the strongest public bridge between acquisition and
  activation. Its former growth lead reported that guided onboarding produced
  nearly twice the activation and referrals of self-serve onboarding, while
  explicitly acknowledging selection bias.
- **HEY** shows the attention value of one coherent point of view, original
  mechanisms, founder credibility, and a complete demonstration. Its
  125,000-signup launch figure still does not reveal retained paid use.
- **MailMate** shows that extreme product and audience specificity can sustain
  a niche through community recommendations and integrations without broad
  search or paid acquisition.
- **Shortwave** shows how an identifiable switching event, a taught workflow,
  recurring feature launches, migration pages, team invitations, and a free
  product can reinforce one another.
- **Mailbird, Canary, Spark, and eM Client** show how content, stores,
  localization, advertising, bundles, partners, free tiers, and business
  routes accumulate over years. They also show why raw scale is misleading:
  much of their modeled search visibility comes from generic email education
  or provider-login help rather than people choosing a client.

For Tecotype, neither confirmed-pain query qualifies as a primary paid
acquisition engine. A very small Classic Outlook test can still measure
whether successful accountless local retrieval leads the same person to
connect live mail, activate the broader client, pay, and retain. It is
Windows-specific learning, not a scalable paid program. The next acquisition
validation is provider-controlled client-choice placement for the normal
Tecotype build. Raycast remains a possible product integration, not a current
acquisition experiment. Neither route is qualified distribution.

## What appears to support discovery and evaluation

These observations explain visible attention, evaluation, or first-value
mechanics. They do not establish attributable activation-to-paid distribution.

### 1. A memorable reason to switch

The clearest stories contain a mechanism or identity, not a generic claim to
be better:

- HEY rejects the conventional inbox and names its replacement mechanisms.
- Superhuman promises a high-performance workflow for people whose
  responsiveness has economic value.
- Shortwave originally connected the loss of Google Inbox to bundles, todos,
  schedules, and a taught inbox method.
- MailMate names IMAP, keyboard control, search, Markdown, and smart mailboxes
  so precisely that a suitable user can self-select.

Mailbird, Canary, Spark, and eM Client lead more often with broad combinations
of productivity, AI, security, providers, platforms, and teams. Their mature
distribution can support that breadth. It is a poor starting position for
Tecotype.

The strongest confirmed pain remains recovering one known locally present
message that Classic Outlook's search omits after the applicable repair checks
fail. Its paid query is research inventory rather than the selected growth
engine. The other candidate jobs remain research inputs, not parallel
campaigns. “Smart email,” “AI inbox,” and “productivity” are still not
sufficiently testable release stories.

### 2. Acquisition and activation need one owner

Superhuman is the wave's strongest evidence that acquisition does not end at
signup. Mandatory onboarding was expensive and selective, but it taught the
company how customers changed their workflow. That learning was later encoded
into self-serve onboarding.

The other clients provide supporting mechanisms:

- HEY publishes forwarding, send-as, contact-import, and workflow education.
- Shortwave maintains migration pages and teaches an inbox method.
- eM Client paired Postbox discounts with import guidance and product work.
- Spark documents the choice and migration between Classic and Desktop.

These mechanisms reduce uncertainty, but none publishes source-level
activation or retention. Tecotype should treat guided first value as a
learning instrument, not a concierge promise it cannot sustain.

### 3. Several public surfaces can reinforce the same offer

The mature portfolios are useful because their public channels appear to
reinforce a current offer:

- app stores make platform presence and release activity inspectable;
- relevant press and reviews give a buyer independent evaluation;
- comparison and migration pages close a known switching decision;
- provider and integration partners place the product near an existing need;
- repeated launches create new attention moments;
- paid media becomes testable once source-to-value measurement exists; and
- free tiers and trials reduce the cash barrier to evaluation.

The qualification is intent. Mailbird and eM Client attract large modeled
volumes around provider logins. Spark's estimate is dominated by CC/BCC
education. Canary's is dominated by broad how-to pages. These may create
awareness and authority, but public evidence cannot show whether they create
retained paid users.

Tecotype should publish one page that closes one validated job before it
creates an inventory.

### 4. Product-native sharing can be visible and ethically costly

Superhuman has used referral priority and mutual subscription credits.
Shortwave uses team invitations, shared links, and a mandatory sent-mail
signature on Free. Mailbird has used rewards, giveaways, gifting, and
affiliates. Team products naturally create invitations and seat expansion.

These are concrete sharing routes, not proof of attributable paid growth. They
can also damage trust through spam, forced branding, recipient tracking,
contact access, or subsidized users who do not retain.

Tecotype should test a user-controlled referral only after paid retention is
measurable. Credit should depend on the referred person reaching a retained
value milestone. No mandatory signature, contact scraping, automatic
invitation, read receipt, or link tracking is necessary.

### 5. Repeated proof creates durable public discovery

Spark, Shortwave, Mailbird, Canary, and eM Client accumulated platform
launches, product releases, stores, articles, press, and campaigns over years.
MailMate accumulated a narrower but unusually long public development record.

The transferable pattern is a release cadence that gives people something
specific and current to evaluate. It does not prove attributable paid
distribution. Tecotype should not manufacture a launch calendar around minor
features. Once a retained Mac cohort exists, meaningful improvements can
support small, factual launch moments.

## What does not establish an acquisition engine

Across the wave, none of these is sufficient:

- a waitlist or signup count;
- Hacker News points or Product Hunt rank;
- funding;
- an undefined user, business, account, or download claim;
- store ratings or cumulative download bands;
- modeled keywords, ETV, backlinks, or mentions;
- the existence of ad creatives, pixels, or affiliate scripts;
- a free tier, trial, discount, lifetime offer, or guarantee;
- many platforms or frequent releases; or
- an invitation, sent-mail signature, or referral program.

Each can be useful evidence of attention, distribution, or measurement
capability. None proves that a suitable person connected a real mailbox,
received value, returned voluntarily, paid, and renewed.

## Repeated risks

### Reach without buying intent

Four of the largest search portfolios in the wave are dominated by generic
education or provider support. This can make domain-level totals look more
commercial than the underlying queries. The same problem affects backlinks
and mentions, which mix relevant coverage with directories, syndication,
mirrors, coupon pages, and unsafe download sites.

Tecotype should use ranking and traffic estimates as diagnostics. The decision
metric is an attributed first-value, day-seven, or paid user.

### Feature breadth weakens the switching story

Shortwave's calm Inbox successor expanded into agents, teams, model tiers, and
Tasklet. Canary combines AI, calendar, security, teams, and cross-platform
claims. Spark and eM Client package broad productivity suites. Superhuman now
lives inside a larger suite domain.

Breadth can support expansion after a product is established. Before release,
it obscures the reason to trust a new primary inbox. Tecotype should keep MCP
and agent automation in future-work language and lead with one current job.

### Trust is part of conversion

The reports repeatedly found:

- privacy headlines broader than the disclosed data path;
- recipient tracking positioned beside privacy protection;
- consent controls and policy language that do not align cleanly;
- undefined or conflicting audience totals;
- lifetime or one-time offers whose update rights require qualification;
- platform pages with stale or conflicting release states;
- migration between overlapping client generations; and
- activation or subscription expiry that can interrupt access.

These are not merely policy or support concerns. An email client asks for
mailbox authorization and habitual use, so ambiguity directly raises
acquisition friction.

Tecotype should publish a current capability matrix, data-flow explanation,
price and entitlement contract, known limitations, release history, and clean
installation/update instructions. Planned work must remain visibly planned.

### Platform breadth hides separate product risks

Every mature client can advertise several platforms because it has already
built distribution, support, release, and migration systems. The existence of
shared product code does not make those systems equivalent.

Tecotype's public manifest and Apple Silicon artifact were verified on
2026-07-19. No public Windows or Linux artifact was verified. GTM planning
assumes eventual readiness on all three desktop platforms, while public copy
and experiment execution remain gated on the actual named-platform build and
the accountless Outlook-search capability.

### Public measurement stops before economics

Mailbird, Superhuman, Shortwave, Spark, and eM Client expose extensive source
or campaign instrumentation. That proves measurement capability, not correct
attribution. No public evidence connects a source through:

```text
visit
→ acquisition handoff
→ first launch
→ mailbox connection
→ first completed sync
→ message-matched first value
→ voluntary return
→ payment
→ renewal
```

Tecotype needs that chain internally with the smallest practical first-party
dataset. It does not need a mature competitor's pixel stack to learn which
first channel works.

### Price without comparable economics

The wave contains free tiers, quarterly support subscriptions, annual and
monthly plans, per-seat business tiers, one-time licenses, update add-ons, and
a $99/year provider. Entitlements, audiences, product maturity, support
burden, and switching cost differ too much for a valid price comparison.

No dossier exposes enough retention, renewal, refund, provider-maintenance,
support, or contribution-margin data to challenge Tecotype's decided
**€149/year or €15/month** position. Tecotype should test whether a complete
professional experience earns that price, not move down-market from public
competitor offers.

## Historical recommended Tecotype sequence

This sequence is preserved as the original Wave 2 conclusion. Its undecided
lead-job and Mac-first ordering were superseded on 2026-07-19. The current hard
gate and verdict are in the
[acquisition index](./readme.md#one-hard-gate).

### Now: choose one frequent job

Run 8–10 problem interviews across:

- several Gmail or Workspace accounts;
- Gmail plus another IMAP provider;
- keyboard-oriented Mac users; and
- people who recover messages from partial or cross-language memory.

Ask for a recent incident, current workaround, frequency, failure cost,
switching attempt, and trust requirement. Show only clearly labeled
development material.

Proceed with one lead job only if at least five participants independently
describe the same frequent problem and at least three recently tried to solve
it.

### Before public acquisition: complete the Mac release gate

The gate should include:

- coherent first run and no-account behavior;
- accurate website, screenshots, requirements, and capability matrix;
- licensing, payment, cancellation, and entitlement behavior;
- Gmail restricted-API/audit completion;
- signed, notarized, updateable Apple Silicon distribution;
- safe and predictable compose, reply, and send behavior;
- claim-by-claim privacy and security verification; and
- privacy-minimal source-to-activation measurement.

MCP, agent automation, and Windows availability remain future or planned work.

### First released test: one source, one job, guided first value

Invite a small source-tagged Mac cohort from the community closest to the
validated job. State affiliation, platform and provider scope, price, data
path, and limitations. Let people choose guided or self-serve setup and record
the selection bias.

Measure:

```text
qualified session
→ signed acquisition
→ first launch
→ account connected
→ first completed sync
→ message-matched search or triage value
→ voluntary day-one return
→ voluntary day-seven return
→ paid conversion
```

Do not broaden acquisition unless at least five non-team users reach first
value and at least three return voluntarily on day seven. These are learning
thresholds, not statistical proof.

### Second test: one complete proof and a small reviewer brief

Turn the validated job into a complete demonstration and a factual brief for a
small number of relevant Mac, keyboard-workflow, search, or privacy-literate
reviewers:

- one-sentence job and category;
- shipped capability matrix;
- data-flow explanation;
- keyboard or Better-search demonstration;
- provider and platform limits;
- price and update rights;
- clean installation and update instructions; and
- no request for editorial control.

HEY supplies the demonstration lesson, MailMate the specificity lesson, and
Superhuman the first-value lesson.

### Third test: one high-intent switching page

Publish one dated page around the winning job. A competitor comparison is
appropriate only when it helps a real decision and accurately states both
products' current providers, platforms, data paths, and prices.

Search impressions and rank are diagnostics. The success metric is an
attributed first-value, day-seven, or paid user. Do not build generic email
education until one high-intent page contributes retained use.

The first concrete version of this historical page can be
`/superhuman-unified-inbox-alternative`, aimed only at Superhuman users or
evaluators with several active accounts. Do not buy the exact query. At 40
modeled US searches/month and USD 15.19 CPC, it needs 24.90% click-to-paid to
meet the USD 61 media-CAC gate. A dated organic comparison and peer
recommendations remain secondary hypotheses. The free result must include
unlimited supported accounts, full All Accounts actions, ordinary
cross-account search and shortcuts, account-specific views, and visible
sender identity. Paid value begins only at a distinct advanced workflow.

Spark and Mailspring are strong free incumbents. Stop for sender mistakes, a
core-action paywall, maintenance cost above the CAC gate, or a current
Superhuman release that removes the gap. This remains Tecotype-owned organic
acquisition, not qualifying distribution.

### Fourth test: one partner or user-controlled referral

After paid conversion and renewal are visible, test either:

- one narrow integration/provider/reviewer partnership whose audience matches
  the validated job; or
- a modest, user-controlled referral credit earned after the referred person
  reaches retained value.

Compare activation, day-seven return, support cost, paid conversion, and
renewal with the direct cohort. Stop if the route creates low-intent volume,
spam, hidden subsidy, or trust complaints.

### Windows: repeat the evidence cycle after it ships

Once signed Windows packaging, updates, first run, provider setup, payment,
safe sending, accessibility, and support pass their own gate, repeat the
guided and self-serve cohort with Windows-specific copy, screenshots,
shortcuts, and source tags.

Mac evidence can inform the hypothesis. It cannot prove Windows availability,
activation, or retention.
